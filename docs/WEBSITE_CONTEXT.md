# Website context

## Concept

This is Igor's personal website.

It displays his values, personality, writing, work, games, and interests. It should function as the digital equivalent of a personal business card for:

- meeting compatible and interesting people;
- professional networking;
- job opportunities;
- possible collaborations;
- sharing games, writing, and other artifacts.

The site should feel like:

> A quick glimpse into Igor's personal notebook.

It is not primarily a conventional portfolio, résumé website, corporate landing page, or personal-brand funnel.

The visitor should first encounter a person, then understand his capabilities, and then inspect the evidence.

## Intended feeling

The site should feel:

- handmade;
- whimsical;
- alive;
- warm;
- playful;
- thoughtful;
- imperfect in an intentional way;
- technically credible;
- professional in an artistic rather than corporate way.

Adventure Time is an important emotional and visual influence, particularly its combination of playfulness, warmth, strangeness, humour, and sincerity.

Do not copy its visual assets or directly reproduce its style.

## Main objectives

The website should help visitors understand:

- who Igor is;
- what he values;
- how he thinks;
- what he has built;
- what kinds of problems he can solve;
- what he is currently exploring;
- how to contact him.

It should support job searching without feeling as though every element exists only to sell Igor to an employer.

## Professional positioning

A broader personal-site description may be:

Do not make the opening so abstract that a hiring manager cannot understand what professional work Igor can do.

## Career model

Do not present job titles as personal identities.

A title is a conventional name for an approximate package of capabilities and responsibilities.

A project combines capabilities to provide an artifact, behaviour, experience, or new capability to another person.

The site should communicate evidence and capabilities rather than trying to define Igor permanently as:

- a backend developer;
- a game developer;
- a data engineer;
- a generalist;
- a creator;
- an architect.

Backend engineering is Igor's strongest proven professional anchor.

Game development is serious recent work and an important personal practice.

They should remain visibly distinct while their overlapping capabilities may be explained where relevant.

## Visual language

The site should use a clean and readable foundation with a handmade layer (the style is called hand-drawn).

Regular prose uses a readable hand-drawn style. Large headings use a distinct notebook-editorial face that remains compatible with the handmade surface. Labels and technical annotations may use a restrained monospace face.

Page navigation should resemble turning to another notebook page. The route-level transition may use perspective and page movement, must not block direct navigation, and must become static when reduced motion is requested.

Games intentionally changes the visual surface to square pixels, stepped motion, pixel borders, and a dark game-like palette. It remains part of the same website through shared navigation, content clarity, and route behavior.

Possible elements include:

- handwritten annotations;
- real sketches;
- brush strokes;
- imperfect lines;
- hand-drawn borders;
- crossed-out or rewritten details;
- slightly misaligned compositions;
- paper or notebook-like fragments;
- small personal illustrations;
- selective visual irregularity.
- pictures of me or something else (would be cool if they would look like stiched like with a tape or something)
- all interactable elements ideally should have an animation in a way of drawing progressing like opening or closing if we remove the mouse from it

The site should feel intentionally imperfect, not unfinished.

Use actual personal marks where possible rather than relying only on generic hand-drawn assets.

## Typography

- Use a readable hand-drawn typeface for regular body text.
- Use a distinct notebook-editorial typeface for headings.
- Reserve looser handwriting treatments for annotations and accents.
- Do not use difficult handwriting for long paragraphs.
- Use a clear monospace font for code and technical material.
- Maintain readable sizing, line height, and contrast.

## Games visual treatment

The Games section may have a distinct pixel-art treatment.

Possible elements include:

- pixel-art frames;

When hovering or focusing on a game card:

- animation may become more active;
- additional details may appear;
- the card may feel as though it has entered an active state.

Opening a game may use one short transition, such as:

- pixel dissolve;
- screen wipe;
- level-entry effect;
- brief sprite-based loading moment.

The transition must:

- remain fast;
- respect reduced-motion preferences;
- work without sound;
- degrade gracefully;
- preserve direct linking;
- not block mobile navigation.

The Games section should remain visibly part of the same website.

## Interaction rules

Custom interactions should:

- create delight;
- reveal personality;
- reinforce the content;
- remain responsive;
- support keyboard and touch interaction.

They should not:

- hide essential information;
- make navigation unpredictable;
- delay access to content;
- depend only on hover;
- interfere with accessibility;
- become demonstrations of technical cleverness without user value.

One strong signature interaction is preferable to many unrelated effects.

## Writing voice

Public writing should be:

- direct;
- personal;
- clear;
- thoughtful;
- playful where appropriate;
- technically credible;
- honest about uncertainty;
- free of generic corporate language.

Avoid:

- inflated claims;
- artificial enthusiasm;
- generic startup phrasing;
- motivational slogans;
- "passionate developer";
- "innovative solutions";
- "10x engineer";
- "AI expert";
- claims of being able to build anything;
- em dashes.

Prefer concrete evidence over adjectives.

## Constraints

- Do not invent additional visual systems.
- Do not add animation without a content purpose.
- Do not overengineer.
- Preserve accessibility.
- Preserve performance.
- Publish before expanding.
- Do not delay the site for a perfect design system.
- Do not redesign everything because one section is imperfect.
- Do not turn the site into a substitute for job outreach.
- Allow the website to become more personal over time.
